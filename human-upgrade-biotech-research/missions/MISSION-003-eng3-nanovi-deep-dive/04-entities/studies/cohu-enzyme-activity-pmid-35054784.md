# Effect of Humid Air Exposed to IR Radiation on Enzyme Activity

## Study Overview

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Design | In vitro enzyme study |
| Intervention | Humid air irradiated with `1270 nm` infrared light, termed `coherent humidity (CoHu)` |
| Enzymes tested | Alkaline phosphatase, horseradish peroxidase, catalase |
| Key question | Whether CoHu can protect or partially restore enzyme activity after damage |
| Affiliations | Emanuel Institute of Biochemical Physics; Lomonosov Moscow State University |

## Key Findings

- One-minute CoHu treatment partially protected enzymes from heat inactivation and mixed-function oxidation.
- The study supports the plausibility of altered aqueous-media behavior as an intermediary mechanism.
- The paper is the clearest peer-reviewed anchor for Eng3’s broader claims about ordered water, coherent humidity, and protein-function support.

## Strengths

1. Peer-reviewed and indexed in PubMed.
2. Directly investigates the specific mechanistic concept Eng3 uses in marketing language.
3. Provides a citable bridge between the company’s water/protein-folding narrative and an experimental system.

## Limitations

1. This is not a human trial.
2. The work studies enzymes in vitro, not clinical outcomes in people using NanoVi devices.
3. Mechanistic plausibility does not establish disease-treatment efficacy or reproduce the full real-world device context.

## Relevance to NanoVi

This paper is best interpreted as `mechanistic support`, not proof of clinical effectiveness. It strengthens the scientific plausibility of the Eng3 narrative but does not validate the full health, recovery, or athletic claims made for the product line.

## Evidence Quality Rating

**Level: Preliminary**

Strong mechanistic relevance, but indirect for real-world clinical claims.
